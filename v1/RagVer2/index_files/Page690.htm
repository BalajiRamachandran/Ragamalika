Testimonial

To be an excellent Carnatic musician is more than most would aspire to in their lifetimes.  To take that further and be both an excellent violinist and an excellent vocalist is to accomplish the work of two lifetimes in one.  To be an excellent teacher on top of that is to multiply the work of those two lifetimes exponentially by extending it to countless others, which is truly and simply invaluable.  And that, to me, is what Maragatham aunty does on a daily basis and without a second thought.   

 

One can go on for pages about her musical accomplishments (and, indeed, one should), but I am most captivated by her equally laudable ability to teach.  Such ability can only be seen in the small gestures and behaviors, and can only be fully appreciated by the student: for example, in the way that her ear can pick up a single flat note by a single individual amidst a group of thirty, and still not point them out directly. 

 

In the way that she is willing, at all times, to bet any amount of money that you WILL reach that note or get that sangathi if you try one more time.  In the way that even as she is making you coffee, she is humming to an imaginary sruthi (perhaps the sruthi of the tube light buzzing above, which I am convinced she can hear distinctly).  In the way that she makes you think she is superhuman in her abilities and yet could not be any more humanistic in her approach. 

 

In the way that she has never ended a class without creating a sense of accomplishment not there an hour ago.  In the way that she spends entire days with her students before concerts, not only teaching and fine-tuning endlessly, but even feeding meals in-between.  In the way that she both humbles you and inspires you with her expectations of greatness.  In the way that although she can lead by presence alone, she chooses instead to lead by example. 

 

And lead she does.  She led me, when I timidly entered the world of Carnatic music thinking only to learn the violin, to learn vocal as well.  She encouraged me to sing and play louder so as to hear my mistakes, not shy away from them.  And then, of course, she nudged me gently to correct them.  She challenged me to sing with my violin and play with my voice.  Both are instruments, and both can be mastered.  She taught me how to sing before she taught me how to play.  But more importantly, she taught me how to listen before she taught me how to sing. 

 

Since I began taking classes with her almost ten years ago, she has repeatedly rendered me awe-struck by her talent, her insight, and ultimately, her passion.  It is rare to find these qualities in both a musician and a teacher, and I am sure her many students will join me when I say that her presence in Northern Virginia has been nothing short of a blessing.        

 

Deeptha Mathavan

Student
